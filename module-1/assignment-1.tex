\noindent\textbf{Problem 1:}  Show how nonnegative integers can be represented in an imaginary ternary computer using trits $(0,1,2)$ instead of bits $(0,1)$. Why don't we do things this way?

\noindent\textbf{Answer:}
Nonnegative integers can be represented in ternary in the same way that they are represented in other bases. Decimal, binary, ternary, octal, hexadecimal, and other similar number systems are all position-based number systems, where each position represents a power of the base of the number system. For a base $b$, a number is represented by a sequence of $n+1$ numbers in the format
\[a_{n} \cdot b^{n},a_{n-1} \cdot b^{n-1},\ldots,a_{1} \cdot b^{1},a_{0} \cdot b^{0},\]
where \[a \in \mathbb{Z},\ 0 \le a < b.\]
The number represented by this sequence is the sum of the elements of the sequence. For example, in decimal, we are working with base-$10$ numbers. Setting $b=10$, we can represent a number in base 10 as:
\begin{align*}
      & a_{n} \cdot b^{n},a_{n-1} \cdot b^{n-1},\ldots,a_{1} \cdot b^{1},a_{0} \cdot b^{0}     \\
  =\  & a_{n} \cdot 10^{n},a_{n-1} \cdot 10^{n-1},\ldots,a_{1} \cdot 10^{1},a_{0} \cdot 10^{0} \\
\end{align*}
The number itself represented by this sequence is usually written without the commas and without the base explicitly noted. If there is ambiguity in context about the base being used, it is shown with a single subscript at the end of the number. Hence, the expression above is simplified as follows:

\begin{align*}
      & a_{n} \cdot 10^{n},a_{n-1} \cdot 10^{n-1},\ldots,a_{1} \cdot 10^{1},a_{0} \cdot 10^{0} \\
  =\  & a_{n},a_{n-1},\ldots,a_{1},a_{0}                                                       \\
\end{align*}

Hence the familiar decimal notation: $1234$, which can alternatively be written $1234_{10}$. Binary and ternary follow the same scheme.

Binary:
\begin{align*}
      & a_{n} \cdot 2^{n},a_{n-1} \cdot 2^{n-1},\ldots,a_{1} \cdot 2^{1},a_{0} \cdot 2^{0} \\
  =\  & a_{n},a_{n-1},\ldots,a_{1},a_{0}                                                   \\
\end{align*}

Ternary:
\begin{align*}
      & a_{n} \cdot 3^{n},a_{n-1} \cdot 3^{n-1},\ldots,a_{1} \cdot 3^{1},a_{0} \cdot 3^{0} \\
  =\  & a_{n},a_{n-1},\ldots,a_{1},a_{0}                                                   \\
\end{align*}

Numbers in binary and ternary might be represented in the following way to disambiguate them from their decimal equivalents.

\[
10_2 \ne 10_{10} \ne 10_3
\]

From the preceding, we can see that these numerical representations to each other mathematically, but each has slightly different properties. Some of the bases are easily convertible to each other, for example when one base is an integer power of another (i.e., $log_{b_1}b_2 \in \mathbb{Z}$), in which case conversion between bases can be done by grouping digits. This is the easiest strategy when converting between binary and hexadecimal, for example. Such conversion is possible but requires more steps between ternary and the common bases used in computer science, such as octal and hexadecimal. It might be objected that this obstacle is a red herring, since there are other bases that have an analogous position to ternary as octal and hexadecimal have to binary (such as base 9 and 27). However, there are other disadvantages to ternary as a number system for classical computing. What is such a disadvantage? In a word, ternary introduces complexity to physical implementations of classical computers, and binary can be used to represent integers (and other numbers) with less physical complexity.

The larger the base, the more numbers can be expressed with a single position. This makes reading the numbers more and less familiar with respect to our default of base 10 from everyday life. However, more information is required to represent numbers of higher bases. For each position in a number's representation, we must choose among $b$ options for the value of $a$. In the case of binary, when constructing a number, we have to make one decision with only two options, viz. $(0,1)$. For ternary, we must make a decision with three options, viz. $(0,1,2)$. This has implications for physical implementations in hardware, since we would need to have a mechanism that could unambiguously be in one of three distinct states to represent each trit, as opposed to the two distinct states to represent bits in binary. It might be physically possible to create a system where each component has three distinct states, but this would impose additional complexity on the physical implementation. As shown above, integers can be converted among any pair of bases. So, we are incentivized to implement this using the simplest option available. This option is binary, because there are only two states that need to be distinguished from each other. What's more, by using binary as the implementation for our computer systems, we are able to take advantage of the simple mapping between bits and boolean values. This mapping means that the two states required in physical representations of binary numbers can also be used for creating digital logic circuits.
